Here's a brief history: - 2002: The Nigerian government sets up a committee to draft a bill for an anti-corruption agency. - 2003: The EFCC Act is passed, and the agency is established on May 13th. - 2003: Nuhu Ribadu is appointed as the first Chairman of the EFCC. - 2004: The EFCC begins operations, focusing on high-profile cases and recovering stolen funds. - 2007: The EFCC arrests and prosecutes several high-profile politicians and business leaders. - 2008: The EFCC establishes a forensic laboratory and a training academy. - 2010: Farida Waziri becomes the second Chairman of the EFCC. - 2011: The EFCC launches a whistleblower policy to encourage citizens to report financial crimes. - 2012: Ibrahim Lamorde becomes the third Chairman of the EFCC. - 2015: Ibrahim Magu becomes the acting Chairman of the EFCC. WHAT DOES EFCC DO

The Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC) performs several functions to combat financial crimes in Nigeria. Some of the key activities include: 1. Investigations: EFCC investigates financial crimes, such as money laundering, fraud, bribery, and corruption. 2. Prosecution: EFCC prosecutes individuals and organizations suspected of financial crimes. 3. Asset recovery: EFCC recovers stolen assets, including properties, cars, and cash. 4. Financial intelligence: EFCC gathers and analyzes financial intelligence to identify suspicious transactions. 5. Public awareness: EFCC educates the public about financial crimes and their impact. 6. Collaboration: EFCC partners with international agencies to combat cross-border financial crimes.
